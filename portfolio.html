10 Personal Statement

In My Own Words


I've always been drawn to questions that don't have clean answers — how do you make something fly? How do you turn an idea into a business? How do you build software that actually helps people? What excites me about engineering is that these questions demand you to be technically capable and creatively relentless at the same time.



Growing up in Sydney, I was always the kid who needed to know how things worked — not from a textbook, but by taking them apart and trying to put them back together differently. That instinct eventually grew into something more structured: I started building. I built an electric go-kart from scratch, working through the physics of motor selection, the practicalities of battery placement, and the humbling reality that real engineering involves a lot of failure before it works. I designed and launched a model rocket, calculating aerodynamic stability before I'd ever formally studied fluid dynamics. I didn't wait for school to teach me these things.

At the same time, I started building software. Today I have six live, deployed web applications — platforms for education, AI integration, student communities, and commercial use. I built a chatbot for a real temple in Artarmon and a full website for a coaching business in Sydney. These weren't hypothetical; they were real deployments for real users. I founded a business, Lumina Corporation, which generates actual revenue from web development services. I'm not describing what I plan to do — I'm describing what I'm already doing.

What draws me to engineering at university isn't just the subject matter — it's the rigour. I want to be in an environment where the problems are hard enough that you can't brute-force your way through them. I want to learn thermodynamics properly, understand structural analysis at depth, and develop the kind of systems thinking that lets you see how all the pieces fit together. I want to study alongside people who are just as driven, and to have access to the facilities, mentorship, and networks that will let me do work that matters at scale.

Entrepreneurship isn't a side interest for me — it's how I think about everything. Every project I build starts with "what problem does this solve?" and ends with "how do you grow it?" The Macquarie Incubator experience showed me that university isn't just about credentials; it's about being embedded in an ecosystem of people building serious things. That's where I want to be.

I'm not applying to university to find out what I'm capable of. I already know I'll build whatever I put my mind to. I'm applying because I want to operate at the highest possible level — and that requires the best possible foundation.

Kaushal Mishra
